# Week 1: Fundamental of Economics

#### 1. Core Concepts and Definitions
*   **The Root Cause of Economics:** The root cause of economic problems is the concept of scarcity. Scarcity occurs because humans have unlimited needs but limited resources.
*   **Definition of Economics:** Economics is basically the optimization under the constraint variable. It is the study of how to allocate limited resources to fulfill unlimited human needs with maximum satisfaction.
*   **Economics vs. Economy:** Economics is a theoretical background involving research, studying correlations, and dynamics between variables. Economy is the production environment where these theoretical models come into reality and money flow begins.
*   **Economics as a Social Science:** Economics is called a social science because parameters change with respect to geographical location, human mindset, age, and gender. Unlike deterministic science (e.g., 1 + 1 = 2), economic responses vary, resulting in scattered plots during surveys.

#### 2. Classification of Economics
*   **Microeconomics:** This involves "zooming in" to focus on individual entities, single companies, or individual user behavior. Examples include analyzing a single Swiggy user's purchase history to provide recommendations or studying the demand for shoes at a specific Adidas shop in a particular town.
*   **Macroeconomics:** This involves "zooming out" to study the whole system or larger sets of data, such as national GDP, unemployment rates, or nationwide successful UPI transactions. A government implementing a nationwide labor law for all delivery platforms is a macroeconomic analysis.

#### 3. Economic Systems
*   **Command (Socialist) Economy:** This is a rule-based system governed by central authorities or the government. The government dictates what to produce, when to produce, and how to produce, acting like a "dictator" with rigid boundaries.
*   **Free Market (Capitalist) Economy:** Individual companies or business owners have the authority to make decisions on pricing, innovations, and discounts with minimal government interference. For example, Reliance launching free incoming calls in 2003 was a company decision, not a government one.
*   **Mixed Economy:** This is a combination of free market innovation and government restrictions or rules. For example, while delivery companies can decide to offer 10-minute delivery (capitalist), the government or Supreme Court may intervene to set safety restrictions for delivery partners (mixed).
*   **Open vs. Closed Economy:**
    *   **Open Economy:** A system where anyone can come, invest, and trade across borders, such as the collaboration between India and Russia.
    *   **Closed Economy:** A system with no exchange outside its boundaries, requiring all manufacturing and technology to be developed in-house, which can make innovation more expensive and time-consuming.

#### 4. Factors of Production
1.  **Land:** The basic requirement needed to build a company, start a startup, or lease a building for operations.
2.  **Labor:** Workers or skilled labor required to build infrastructure and run day-to-day operations.
3.  **Physical Capital:** This is divided into two types:
    *   **Fixed Capital:** Long-term investments such as land, buildings, production line machines, and power generators.
    *   **Working Capital:** Short-term or monthly investments for day-to-day operations, including employee salaries, electricity bills, and raw materials.
4.  **Entrepreneurship (Human Capital):** The management or individuals (like CEOs and senior managers) who coordinate and manage the other resources (land, labor, and capital).

#### 5. Sectors of Production
*   **Primary Sector:** Known as the "red color workforce," it focuses on raw materials coming from mining or natural resources. It contributes approximately 17% to the total GDP.
*   **Secondary Sector:** Known as the "blue collar workforce," this is where manufacturing and production activities occur after consuming raw materials. It contributes nearly 27% of GDP.
*   **Tertiary Sector:** Known as the "white collar workforce," this sector bridges the gap between the factory and the consumer through logistics, supply chain, and quality management. It dominates the economy, contributing 54% of GDP.
*   **Quaternary and Quinary Sectors:** The quaternary sector involves R&D and innovation activities, while the quinary sector involves high-level strategic decision-making by company authorities.

#### 6. Key Economic Principles and Mathematical Applications
*   **Trade-off:** The concept of giving up one opportunity to pursue another.
*   **Opportunity Cost:** The value of what is lost when choosing one alternative over another.
*   **Sunk Cost:** Costs that have already been incurred and cannot be recovered, such as time lost sitting in stagnant traffic.
*   **Optimization Example (Ramu's Tea Stall):**
    *   **Constraint Equation:** $6C + 12F = 1200$ (where $C$ is cups of tea and $F$ is cups of filter coffee).
    *   If Ramu receives a fixed order for 40 filter coffees ($F = 40$), the remaining budget allows him to produce 120 cups of tea ($C = (1200 - 12(40)) / 6 = 120$).
    *   **Net Profit Calculation:** With a profit of ₹4 per tea and ₹6 per coffee, total profit is $(4 \times 120) + (6 \times 40) = 720$.

*   **Production Possibility Frontier (PPF):** A curve showing the proper utilization of resources. Points on the curve represent efficiency, points inside represent under-utilization (low efficiency), and points outside are overshooting the current resource capacity.

#### 7. The Three Central Problems of Economics
Every firm or startup must address three fundamental questions:
1.  **What to produce?** Determining market demand and if there is a need for the product.
2.  **How to produce?** Deciding on the resources and production activities needed, such as using biodiesel resources.
3.  **Whom to produce for?** Identifying the potential customers for the product.
