### 1. Graphical Representation in Economics
Graphs are used to visualize patterns, express ideas, and identify trends in data that might not be easily interpreted from mathematical equations or theories alone.

#### Mathematical vs. Economic Perspective
*   **Mathematics:** Typically uses static plots focusing on a single dependent variable and an independent variable (e.g., $y = mx + c$).
*   **Economics:** Variables are often interconnected and dynamic in nature, meaning they change with respect to time. In many economic models, both variables on the axes are dependent on one another.
    *   **Example (Price and Quantity):** If the price of a commodity (like bananas) increases, the quantity demanded typically decreases. Conversely, if a consumer increases their volume purchase, they may receive a discount, changing the price.

#### Core Concepts of Slopes and Correlation
*   **Slope:** Represents the responsiveness of change in variables.
    $$\text{Slope} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$$
*   **Positive Correlation (Upward Sloping):** When one variable increases, the other also increases. For example, as study time increases, the possibility of achieving higher grades increases.
*   **Negative Correlation (Downward Sloping):** When one variable increases, the other decreases. For example, as "party time" increases, grades tend to decrease.

### 2. Cost of Production and Profit
Cost of production involves the investment required to procure inputs—such as raw materials, machines, land, labor, and facilities—to convert them into outputs.

#### Fundamental Equations
*   **Total Revenue ($TR$):** The total amount received from selling products in the market.
    $$TR = \text{Quantity} \times \text{Selling Price}$$
*   **Profit:**
    $$\text{Profit} = \text{Total Revenue} - \text{Cost of Production}$$

### 3. The Production Function
The production function defines the relationship between inputs and the resulting output.

#### Cobb-Douglas Equation
A generalized equation used to calculate the production function based on input variables:
$$Q = X_1^{\alpha} \cdot X_2^{\beta}$$
(Where $Q$ is quantity/output, $X_1$ and $X_2$ are input factors of production, and $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are constants).

#### Short-run vs. Long-run Production
*   **Short-run:** A period where at least one factor of production (often land or factory space) is fixed. Production can only be increased by varying other inputs, like labor.
*   **Long-run:** A period where all factors of production are variable.

### 4. Short-run Cost Analysis
Total cost is the summation of fixed and variable costs.

*   **Total Fixed Cost ($TFC$):** Costs that do not change with the quantity of production in the short run (e.g., rent, insurance, salaries).
*   **Total Variable Cost ($TVC$):** Costs that change directly with production levels. If production is zero, variable cost is zero.
*   **Average Total Cost ($ATC$):** The per-unit cost of output.
    $$ATC = \frac{\text{Total Cost}}{\text{Quantity}}$$
*   **Average Fixed Cost ($AFC$):**
    $$AFC = \frac{\text{Total Fixed Cost}}{\text{Quantity}}$$
    Note: $AFC$ continuously declines as quantity increases but never reaches zero.
*   **Marginal Cost ($MC$):** The additional cost incurred to produce one extra unit of output.
    $$MC = \frac{\Delta \text{Total Cost}}{\Delta \text{Quantity}}$$

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Law of Diminishing Marginal Production:** Adding more of a variable input (like workers) to a fixed resource (like a kitchen) eventually results in smaller increases in output. This occurs because resources become crowded or over-utilized.

### 5. Opportunity Cost and Sunk Cost
*   **Opportunity Cost:** The value of the next best alternative that is given up when making a choice.
    *   *Example:* Choosing to attend a lecture instead of working a freelancing job that pays $\$100$/hour makes the $\$100$ your opportunity cost.
*   **Sunk Cost:** An expenditure that has already been incurred and cannot be recovered (e.g., non-refundable tickets or time already lost in traffic). Sunk costs should not influence future rational decisions.

### 6. The Profit Illusion: Economic vs. Accounting Profit
There is a critical distinction between how accountants and economists view profit based on the types of costs they include.

#### Cost Types
*   **Explicit Costs:** Actual out-of-pocket cash payments for running the business (wages, rent, raw materials).
*   **Implicit Costs:** The non-monetary opportunity costs associated with using resources the owner already owns (e.g., the value of the owner's time, forgone salary from a previous job, or the rent that could be earned from leasing one's own land).

#### Profit Calculations
*   **Accountant's View:** Only considers explicit cash flows.
    $$\text{Accounting Profit} = \text{Total Revenue} - \text{Explicit Costs}$$
*   **Economist's View:** Considers both explicit and implicit (opportunity) costs.
    $$\text{Economic Profit} = \text{Total Revenue} - (\text{Explicit Costs} + \text{Implicit Costs})$$

#### Strategic Interpretations of Economic Profit ($EP$)
*   **$EP = 0$ (Normal Profit):** The business is stable/in equilibrium; it covers all opportunity costs.
*   **$EP > 0$:** The business is in a growth space; it can expand or attract new firms.
*   **$EP < 0$:** The business is not utilizing resources efficiently compared to alternatives; it may be better to exit the market.

### 7. Production Possibility Frontier (PPF)
The PPF is a curve showing the maximum possible output combinations of two goods or services a business can produce using its available resources efficiently.

#### Efficiency on the PPF
*   **On the Curve:** Resources are fully and efficiently utilized.
*   **Inside the Curve:** Resources are underutilized (inefficiency).
*   **Outside the Curve:** Unattainable with current resources; requires economic growth or technological advancement.

#### Marginal Rate of Transformation ($MRT$)
The slope of the PPF measures the opportunity cost of producing one good in terms of the other.
$$MRT = \frac{\Delta Y (\text{Sacrifice})}{\Delta X (\text{Gain})}$$

#### Shifts and Rotations
*   **Rightward Shift:** Indicates economic growth, increase in manpower, or technological advancement.
*   **Leftward Shift:** Indicates a decrease in resources, such as employees leaving or machine failure.
*   **Rotation:** Occurs when one product's production capability remains fixed while the other changes (e.g., efficiency improves for only one product).

### 8. Optimization Problem Example (Case Study: GreenFleet001)
*   **Budget:** $₹20,000$
*   **Fixed Requirement:** Deploy exactly 10 Electric 3-Wheelers ($E_3$)
*   **Costs:** $E_2 = ₹100$; $E_3 = ₹400$
*   **Profits:** $E_2 = ₹500$; $E_3 = ₹1,500$

**Step 1: Constraint Equation**
$$100E_2 + 400(10) = 20,000$$
$$100E_2 + 4,000 = 20,000 \rightarrow 100E_2 = 16,000$$
$$E_2 = 160 \text{ vehicles}$$

**Step 2: Total Profit**
$$\text{Profit} = (160 \times 500) + (10 \times 1,500)$$
$$\text{Profit} = 80,000 + 15,000 = ₹95,000$$
