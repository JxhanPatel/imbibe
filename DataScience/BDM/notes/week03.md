# Week 3: Business Basics and B2B Strategic Selling

### 1. Understanding Business Basics

#### Customer vs. Consumer
It is important to understand the distinction between a customer and a consumer, as these terms are often used interchangeably but have different meanings in a business context.
*   **Customer:** The entity that pays for the product or service.
*   **Consumer:** The entity that actually uses or consumes the product or service.
*   **Relationship:** A customer can be a consumer (e.g., an individual buying and drinking their own coffee). However, a consumer need not be a customer (e.g., parents paying tuition fees are customers, while the students are the consumers).

#### Marketing vs. Sales
There is often confusion between marketing and sales, though they are distinct functions within an organization.
*   **Marketing:** Associated with creating awareness through promotions and advertisements. It is characterized by a **cash outflow** as the company spends money to create this awareness.
*   **Sales:** Associated with the actual transaction and results in a **cash inflow** to the system.
*   **Selling Definition:** To sell, a minimum of two entities are required: a seller (supplier/service provider) and a buyer (customer/client). The seller sells offerings (products, services, or both) to the buyer, ideally at a profit.

> [!NOTE]
> **American Marketing Association (AMA) Definition:** Marketing involves choosing a target market, getting, keeping, and growing customers, and then creating, communicating, and delivering an attractive value proposition.

#### The Framework of Marketing
Marketing can be summarized through three core frameworks:
1.  **The 4 Ps:** Product, Price, Promotion, and Place.
2.  **The 5 Cs:** Customer, Company, Context, Competitor, and Collaborator.
3.  **STP:** Segmentation, Targeting, and Positioning.

*   **Segmentation:** Grouping customers who have similar needs and wants into clusters.
*   **Targeting:** Identifying and selecting a specific segment to serve, typically based on the "mode" or the segment with the highest frequency of preference in survey data.
*   **Positioning:** How a product or service is positioned in the mind of the consumer to pass a positive signal or message (e.g., "good quality nutritious food at a decent price").

### 2. Profit and Loss Dynamics
The primary objective of any selling process, whether B2C or B2B, is to sell at a profit for company sustainability.

*   **Cost Price (CP):** The total cost incurred in making a product, from procurement of raw materials to finishing.
*   **Selling Price (SP):** The cost at which the product is sold in the market.
*   **Profit:** Occurs when $SP > CP$.
*   **Loss:** Occurs when $SP < CP$.
*   **Break-even:** Occurs when $SP = CP$ (no profit, no loss).

**Minimizing Loss Strategy:** In cases where a product cannot be sold at a profit (e.g., becoming obsolete due to new technology), a company may sell below cost price to minimize the total loss. For example, if a pen costs ₹10 to make and is not sold, the loss is ₹10; if it is sold for ₹5, the loss is reduced to ₹5.

### 3. B2C vs. B2B Differences
Transactions are classified based on the nature of the end customer.

| Feature | B2C (Business to Consumer) | B2B (Business to Business) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **End Customer** | Individual | Business/Organization |
| **Number of Customers** | Very high | Less and geographically concentrated |
| **Supply Chain** | Larger (Manufacturer $\rightarrow$ Distributor $\rightarrow$ Wholesaler $\rightarrow$ Retailer $\rightarrow$ Customer) | Shorter (Direct sales or one intermediary) |
| **Purchase Duration** | Shorter (minutes to days) | Longer (3 months to 1 year) |
| **Purchase Value** | Lesser (e.g., ₹2,000 shirt) | Very high (Millions to Billions) |
| **Relationship** | Short-term, transactional | Long-term, collaborative |
| **Elasticity** | Price Elastic | Price Inelastic (conditional) |
| **Buying Behavior** | Impulse buying is common | No impulse buying |
| **Contracts** | Lower/Fewer agreements | Higher/Extensive contracts |
| **Decision Maker** | Individual/Family/Friends | Decision Making Unit (DMU) (5-15 people) |
| **Jilting Impact** | Lower | Higher |

#### The Jilting Effect
This is the shock or impact a company experiences when a customer changes their mind after advanced negotiations. In B2B, because the number of customers is small and the time invested is high, the impact of a canceled deal is severe compared to B2C.

### 4. Products vs. Services
Offerings are a combination of products and services.

*   **Products:** Tangible items that can be seen, touched, smelled, felt, and stored in inventory.
*   **Services:** Characterized by the **IHIP** framework:
    *   **Intangible:** Cannot be sensed before purchase.
    *   **Heterogeneous:** No two service experiences are exactly the same.
    *   **Inseparable:** Simultaneous production and consumption; the customer must be present at the location.
    *   **Perishable:** Cannot be stored or inventoried (e.g., a missed flight).

#### Evaluation of Services
Evaluation is more difficult for services than products.
*   **Search Attributes:** Can be evaluated prior to purchase (common for products).
*   **Experience Attributes:** Can only be evaluated during or after consumption (e.g., flight travel, attending a class).
*   **Credence Attributes:** Attributes the consumer cannot confidently evaluate even after consumption (e.g., complex surgery, legal services, insurance).

#### Service Quality (ServQual) Dimensions
Services are assessed based on five dimensions: Reliability, Responsiveness, Assurance, Empathy, and Tangibles.

#### Economic Importance of Services
*   Contribute 80% to the GDP of developed economies (e.g., France, US) and approximately 60% in India.
*   A major source of employment generation.
*   Provide a **competitive advantage** and recurring revenue.
*   **Servitization:** Adding services to products (e.g., Rolls Royce "power by the hour").

### 5. Organizational Buying Behavior (OBB)
The B2B purchase process typically follows an **eight-step process**.

1.  **Problem Recognition:** Identifying a problem or a need (e.g., engineers complaining about slow laptops).
2.  **General Description of Needs:** Listing what is required to solve the problem.
3.  **Product Specifications:** Providing technical specs (e.g., Intel i7 processor, 16GB RAM).
4.  **Supplier Search:** Identifying potential vendors via market research.
5.  **Analysis and Acquisition of Proposals:** DMU members analyze quotes or proposals in detail (often through **RFQ** - Request for Quote).
6.  **Supplier Selection:** Selecting a vendor based on **CSF/KSF** (Critical/Key Success Factors) rather than just price.
7.  **Selection of Order Routine:** Finalizing details like color, software installation, and payment terms (installments vs. cash).
8.  **Post-Usage Performance Review:** Assessing if the product solved the problem. This is the only step common to both B2C and B2B.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **RFI vs. RFQ:**
> *   **RFI (Request for Information):** Early stage, gathering general market information to be "doubly sure" before ordering.
> *   **RFQ (Request for Quotation):** Issued when exact technical specifications are determined and vendors are asked for pricing.

#### Mathematical Example: Vendor Rating
A company evaluates vendors by allocating weightages to KSFs that sum to 100% and then rating them on a scale of 1-5.
**Formula:** $\text{Final Score} = \sum (\text{Weightage} \times \text{Rating})$.

*Example for "Turn AB":*
*   Pricing (50% weight): Score 5 $\rightarrow 0.50 \cdot 5 = 2.5$.
*   Service Quality (30% weight): Score 4 $\rightarrow 0.30 \cdot 4 = 1.2$.
*   Brand Image (10% weight): Score 1 $\rightarrow 0.10 \cdot 1 = 0.1$.
*   Market Reputation (10% weight): Score 1 $\rightarrow 0.10 \cdot 1 = 0.1$.
*   **Total Rating:** $2.5 + 1.2 + 0.1 + 0.1 = 3.9$.

### 6. B2B Buying Situations
Not all eight steps are followed in every B2B purchase; it depends on the situation.

*   **Straight Rebuy:** For inexpensive or frequently bought items (e.g., pens, office supplies). Often only steps 7 and 8 are needed.
*   **Modified Rebuy:** When modifications are needed in an existing product (e.g., new software). Following all eight steps depends on the cost of modifications.
*   **New Task Situation:** For expensive products bought for the first time. All eight steps must be followed.

### 7. The Decision Making Unit (DMU)
Also called a **Buying Center** or **Buying and Usage Center (BUC)**, this unit usually comprises 5 to 15 people.

#### Roles in the DMU:
*   **Gatekeeper:** Controls the flow of information; ensures random people do not influence DMU members (e.g., Security, Finance department budget caps, Quality standards).
*   **User:** The entity that actually uses the product/service and often identifies the initial problem.
*   **Initiator:** The person who starts the formal purchase process (e.g., a supervisor or manager).
*   **Influencer:** Individuals (Heads of Departments, Directors) who provide disciplinary expertise and recommendations.
*   **Decision Maker:** The final authority who signs the agreement (CEO, C-suite, or a committee).

**Negative Inheritance of Domino Effect (Spillover):** A wrong B2B decision impacts not just the immediate company, but its entire downstream client network.

### 8. Sales Techniques and Market Platforms
Sales techniques are methods used to persuade or woo customers to buy.

#### Types of Selling:
*   **Direct Selling:** Seller sells directly to the buyer; most prominent in B2B due to smaller customer numbers.
*   **Indirect Selling:** Seller connected to buyer via an intermediary or agent (e.g., Amazon, Caterpillar's distributors).
*   **Wholesaling:** A B2B technique where quantity comes from distributors or producers and is sold to retailers.
*   **Retail Selling:** Selling through stores (e.g., Reliance Fresh, Kirana stores).
*   **Online Selling:** Selling via the internet (e.g., Flipkart).

#### Supply Chain Volume and Profit Margins
In a typical chain (Manufacturer $\rightarrow$ Distributor $\rightarrow$ Wholesaler $\rightarrow$ Retailer $\rightarrow$ Customer):
*   **Volume Hierarchy:** Manufacturer $\ge$ Distributor $>$ Wholesaler $>$ Retailer $>$ Customer.
*   **Profit Distribution:** If a manufacturer makes a bottle for ₹10 and the customer pays ₹20, the manufacturer's profit may only be ₹2 (selling to distributor at ₹12), as margins are split across the chain to cover fixed and varying costs like rent and salaries.

#### Market Environments
*   **Marketplace:** A physical location where buyers and sellers meet for face-to-face negotiations (e.g., Sabzi Mandi, retail stores).
*   **Marketspace:** A virtual space where buyers and sellers are connected via technology (e.g., eBay, Amazon).
